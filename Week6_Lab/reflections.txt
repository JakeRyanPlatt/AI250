Configuration 8 delivered the best overall results, achieving a test accuracy of 86.33% with a test loss of 0.4095, using filters of (64,128,256), dropout of 0.5, 60 epochs, a batch size of 32, and a learning rate of 0.001. Its train and validation accuracies were closely aligned at 85.62% and 85.41% respectively, suggesting a well-generalized model with minimal overfitting. The hyperparameter combination of moderate dropout and extended training appeared to strike the right balance between capacity and regularization.

Across all configurations, increasing the number of epochs generally improved performance up to a point, after which signs of overfitting emerged in some runs. Higher dropout values (0.7) tended to improve validation accuracy relative to training accuracy, which is expected since dropout acts as a regularizer. Larger filter sizes in the convolutional layers, such as (64,128,256), consistently pushed training accuracy higher, but this did not always translate proportionally to test accuracy. Configurations 6 and 7 flagged as errors, likely due to instability or memory issues at larger batch sizes combined with high dropout.

Somewhat surprisingly, config 8 with only 0.5 dropout outperformed several configs using 0.7 dropout despite running more epochs, suggesting that aggressive dropout can limit a model's ability to learn effectively even with extended training time.

Going forward, I would experiment with early stopping to automatically halt training when validation loss plateaus, which could reduce overfitting risk while saving compute time. Trying a learning rate scheduler or reducing lr to 0.0005 after a certain epoch could also help refine convergence. Additionally, testing batch sizes of 16 alongside config 8's architecture might yield further gains in generalization.
